The Two-Speed Bank

Artificial intelligence will not lift Australian financial services evenly. By 2030 it will split the industry in two — a fast lane of AI-native institutions capturing the productivity windfall, and a slow lane of laggards and displaced workers left behind. Which Australia we get is being decided by regulatory choices made right now.

01 — The Claim

The productivity windfall is real. So is the split it will cause.

Australian financial services is one of the most AI-ready industries in one of the most AI-ready economies on earth. That is precisely why its transformation will be uneven — and why the next eighteen months of policy matter more than the technology itself.

The headline numbers are not speculative. Australian financial firms already report using AI at 67% versus 57% globally, and generative AI at 45% versus 31% globally (Broadridge 2025). The Infosys Bank Tech Index put active AI use among Australian financial firms at 76% by mid-2025 (CFOtech 2026). The Productivity Commission's central estimate is that AI and data could add roughly $116 billion to Australian economic activity over the decade, lifting labour productivity by about 4.3% (Productivity Commission 2025).

My thesis is that these aggregate gains conceal a distributional rupture. AI in finance is not a uniform productivity uplift; it is a tool whose returns concentrate among firms with the capital, data and talent to deploy it at scale. The result by 2030 is a two-speed industry: a small cluster of incumbents and AI-native fintechs that compound their advantage, and a long tail — smaller mutuals, regional players, and the workers doing the routine cognitive tasks AI does first — that absorbs the disruption without the gains. The Commonwealth Bank's July 2025 decision to cut 45 call-centre roles after deploying an AI chatbot, then reverse it under union pressure, is the whole forecast in miniature: the technology arrives faster than the institutions designed to share its benefits (University of Wollongong 2026).

02 — Capabilities to Channels

How AI capabilities become economic outcomes

To forecast credibly, you need a mechanism, not a vibe. I use the task-based framework of Acemoglu and Restrepo (2019), which treats production as a bundle of tasks and asks what AI does to each.

In this framework, a new technology acts on the economy through competing forces. The displacement effect substitutes capital for labour in tasks machines can now do, pushing down labour demand and wages. The productivity effect lowers costs and raises output, which can expand demand for labour in non-automated tasks. And the reinstatement effect creates entirely new tasks where labour holds the advantage. Whether workers gain or lose depends on the balance between these forces — and on a fourth dynamic, skill-biased technical change, which raises returns to workers who complement AI while eroding them for those who substitute for it.

Mapping the channels onto finance

Financial services is unusually exposed because so much of its value chain is routine cognitive work — exactly the tasks that large language models and predictive systems now perform. Four channels dominate:

1. Analytical automation (displacement). Credit assessment, compliance review, document processing, first-line customer service and junior research are being automated. McKinsey estimates basic automation makes a team 15–20% faster, with far larger gains where AI takes fuller control (University of Wollongong 2026). These are graduate and mid-tier roles — the traditional entry points into finance careers.

2. Augmentation (productivity). For senior analysts, portfolio managers and advisers, AI is a complement: it drafts, summarises and models faster, raising the output of those who direct it. Australian firms expect AI to have a high impact on productivity (73%) more than on headcount reduction (Broadridge 2025) — consistent with augmentation, not just replacement.

3. New tasks (reinstatement). AI assurance, model-risk governance, prompt and data engineering, and AI-product design are genuinely new finance roles. The question is volume: Acemoglu and Restrepo (2019) argue reinstatement historically lags displacement, leaving a transitional gap in which workers are displaced faster than new tasks emerge.

4. Market structure. AI's returns scale with data and compute, which favours large incumbents and well-funded fintechs. This is the channel most likely to drive the two-speed split — and the one least captured by aggregate productivity numbers.

03 — Three Futures

Baseline, upside, downside

The same technology produces three very different 2030s. What separates them is not how good the models get — it is adoption breadth, the speed of reinstatement, and whether policy redistributes the gains. Toggle between the scenarios below.

Upside · "Broad-based uplift"

Diffusion beats concentration

Adoption spreads beyond the big four to mutuals and regionals via shared infrastructure. Reskilling keeps pace, so displaced workers move into augmented and new roles. Productivity and reinstatement effects dominate; the wage distribution widens only modestly. Key drivers: open-data access, public reskilling investment, competition policy that prevents data moats.

Baseline · "Two-speed"

Gains concentrate, slowly

Incumbents and funded fintechs compound advantages; smaller players lag. Aggregate output rises near the PC central path, but entry-level roles thin and the benefit skews to capital and senior talent. Displacement and reinstatement roughly offset over time, with a painful transitional gap. Most likely absent policy change.

Downside · "Hollowing out"

Displacement runs ahead

Rapid cost-driven automation (the CBA chatbot pattern, repeated) outpaces reskilling and reinstatement. Market power concentrates; consumer harms from opaque models trigger reactive, heavy-handed regulation that further entrenches incumbents who can afford compliance. Key risks: trust collapse, thin entry-level pipeline, regulatory whiplash.

04 — Policy Playbook

The levers that move the trajectory

Australia is at a genuine fork. The Productivity Commission has urged that AI-specific regulation be a "last resort," built on existing legal foundations, and that mandatory high-risk guardrails be paused (ACS 2025). ASIC's Joe Longo signalled the regulator is "not rushing" to new rules but will use existing powers more boldly (ASIC 2025). APRA ran a targeted prudential review of AI across large banks, insurers and super in late 2025 (APRA 2025). The light-touch instinct is defensible for growth — but it is exactly the setting most likely to deliver the two-speed baseline. Expand the branches to see the trade-offs.

Lever 1 — Competition & data access: stop the moats forming

AI returns scale with data, so the single biggest driver of concentration is unequal data access. Strengthening and "rightsizing" the Consumer Data Right so smaller players and customers can actually use their data — a direction the PC itself recommends — lowers the barrier that lets incumbents pull away.

Trade-off: heavier data-sharing mandates raise compliance costs that big firms absorb more easily, which can paradoxically entrench them. Design matters more than intensity.

KPI: share of AI-driven revenue captured outside the big four

Lever 2 — Reskilling & the entry-level pipeline

The displacement effect hits routine cognitive roles — graduate analysts, processing, first-line service — which are also the industry's training ground. If those rungs disappear, the future senior-talent pipeline breaks. Co-funded reskilling and protected apprenticeship-style pathways turn displacement into reinstatement faster.

Trade-off: public reskilling is fiscally costly and slow; firms may free-ride. Tying support to retention commitments helps but reduces flexibility.

KPI: time-to-redeployment for displaced finance workers

Lever 3 — Consumer protection & model governance

Robo-advice, algorithmic credit and AI-driven pricing create real risks of bias and opacity. 77% of Australians believe AI regulation is necessary (KPMG 2025), and trust is the binding constraint on adoption. Using existing ASIC/APRA powers for outcomes-based oversight protects consumers without a slow new AI Act.

Trade-off: too little oversight invites a trust-collapse and reactive over-regulation (the downside path); too much too early raises costs only large firms can bear.

KPI: complaint & remediation rates on AI-mediated products

Lever 4 — Timing & sequencing

The PC's "last resort" stance buys growth but front-loads concentration risk. A sequenced approach — competition and reskilling levers now, while monitoring for consumer harm and tightening governance only as evidence warrants — captures most of the upside while keeping the downside in reach of correction.

Unintended consequence to watch: regulatory whiplash. A trust shock that forces sudden heavy rules would entrench the very incumbents light-touch policy was meant to discipline.

KPI: regulatory-change volatility index

The through-line: every lever that fights concentration also tends to raise compliance costs that incumbents bear best. That tension is the central governance problem of AI in Australian finance. The policy task is not choosing more or less regulation — it is designing interventions whose costs do not themselves become a moat.

05 — Methods, Data & Limitations

Showing my work

Approach. The forecast applies the Acemoglu–Restrepo (2019) task-based framework as its causal engine, decomposing AI's effect into displacement, productivity, reinstatement and skill-bias channels, then mapping each onto financial-services tasks. Scenarios vary three parameters: adoption breadth, reskilling/reinstatement speed, and redistributive policy intensity.

Data. Adoption figures are from the Broadridge (2025) and Infosys (2025) surveys; macro productivity and the $116B/4.3% figures from the Productivity Commission's Harnessing Data and Digital Technology interim and final reports (2025); industry growth potential from AFIA (2025); the CBA case from contemporaneous reporting. Regulatory positions are drawn directly from ASIC, APRA and the PC.

The chart projections are illustrative, not predictive. The scenario lines are indexed model outputs anchored to the PC central estimate to make mechanisms vivid; they are not econometric point forecasts and should not be read as such.

Limitations & uncertainty. Three matter most. First, data gaps: firm-level distribution of AI gains in Australian finance is largely unmeasured, so the concentration thesis rests on mechanism and analogy more than direct data. Second, model limits: the task framework assumes tasks are separable and that reinstatement is possible; a genuine structural break (e.g. agentic AI automating whole functions) would break those assumptions. Third, policy endogeneity: the regulatory settings I treat as drivers are themselves shaped by the outcomes they produce, so the scenarios are paths, not predictions.
